United States

Affordable Care Act

Overview

The National Center for Health Statistics mortality data on maternal deaths in the U.S. from 2009-2019 was collected from the CDC Wonder site.

Total maternal mortality included maternal deaths owing to obstetrical tetanus, maternal deaths up to 42 days after delivery, as well as late maternal deaths (defined by the WHO as the death of a woman from direct or indirect obstetric causes, more than 42 days but less than one year after termination of pregnancy).

The maternal mortality ratio was calculated as the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births, using The National Center for Health Statistics natality data collected from the CDC Wonder site.

Several states did not provide data on maternal deaths between 2009-2019. Between 2003 and 2017, states were incrementally implementing a pregnancy status checkbox on death certificates which affected the comparability of data between states (Hoyert, Uddin, Minino, 2020). By 2017 all states had implemented a checkbox (National Center for Health Statistics, 2020).

States with No Medicaid Expansion

As of 2017, Medicaid coverage was responsible for financing 43% of U.S. births (Martin, Hamilton, Osterman, Driscoll, & Drake, 2018). A study published in February 2020 found that Medicaid expansion was significantly associated with lower maternal mortality when compared to states that had not expanded medicaid (Eliason, 2020).

As of December 2020, twelve states in the U.S. have no ACA Medicaid Expansion: Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin, and Wyoming.

Summary

The U.S. has the worst maternal mortality ratio among high-income nations (Eliason, 2020). Within the U.S. there is a disparity in maternal mortality ratios among states, and access to insurance and medicaid coverage is an important factor. State decisions affect women's access to prenatal and postpartum care, and the resulting outcomes of pregnancy.
